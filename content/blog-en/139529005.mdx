RockMass is working to break into the niche market of mining and tunnel engineering.

This Toronto-based startup is leveraging NVIDIA AI to develop a mapping platform that helps engineers assess the stability of mine shafts and tunnels under construction.

Currently, as a safety precaution, geologists and engineers stand five meters away from rock formations and visually assess the hazard level of rock strata. However, RockMass co-founder and CEO Shelby Yee believes this is not an ideal way to ensure the accuracy of results.

"Using their current assessment method, the entire process takes nearly 90 minutes, whereas with our technology, it can be completed in about 5 minutes," Yee stated.

RockMass is testing its handheld device, Mapper, with field engineers. This device is applied in mining, geological exploration, and civil engineering. The startup is developing an AI platform for robots, drones, and handheld devices that capture geological data.

Now, the startup's Mapper AI device offers a safer way for engineers to stay clear of potentially collapsing tunnels; it also provides a faster data collection and processing system. Robots and drones using this platform can enter higher-risk areas.

RockMass's clients include Brazilian mining company Nexa Resources, which seeks to leverage RockMass's technology to enhance its automation and safety levels.

**AI for Geotechnical Engineering**

For years, engineers have used traditional equipment to measure the angles of rock surfaces, such as optical surveying instruments mounted on tripods, similar to theodolites. They need to identify so-called weak planes to determine fracture points within tunnels and rock formations.

Engineers measure rock surfaces and collect data to construct what is known as a stereonet. A stereonet can represent three-dimensional shapes (such as boulders) on a two-dimensional display.

![](https://pub-048dcb96257f476697b113fcb5939cb9.r2.dev/blog/139529005/02_6bc919e4b1bc38de4231e318382dcd95.png)

Matt Gubasta (Co-founder and CFO) tests the instrument at an underground testing center in Sudbury, Ontario.

Traditionally, engineers would need to bring data collected from the field back to the office and then transfer it to a computer to construct a stereonet.

The startup's technology offers a simpler method. Its handheld device is equipped with sensors that can perform such measurements. Its LiDAR sensors and inertial measurement units can map the orientation of weak planes within rock formations. Furthermore, the device can operate normally even in underground environments without GPS, wireless communication, or light.

By leveraging the information provided by these sensors, RockMass's software can quickly identify usable data for engineers within minutes. The company is dedicated to helping field engineers capture and process on-site data. "We can view the data in real-time," Yee said.

**AI for High-Compute Demands**

According to co-founder and CTO Stuart Bourne, RockMass's platform for collecting field data has very high computational requirements. The company's devices rely on the robotic performance of NVIDIA Jetson, supported by CUDA, cuDNN, and TensorRT software libraries.

"Jetson's computational power is remarkably high relative to its energy consumption," Bourne stated.

The startup utilizes the CUDA library to process data in real-time within cloud instances running NVIDIA GPUs, thereby processing stereonets for clients.

"No one else can collect and process data like we do," Yee stated. "We can process data in the cloud in real-time, thanks to the computational power of GPUs."
