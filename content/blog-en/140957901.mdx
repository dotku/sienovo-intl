## All-Domestic ARM+FPGA+Line Scan Camera: Principles, Technology, and Applications, Customizable

In this article, we will explore the principles, technology, and applications of line scan cameras, particularly focusing on their advantages over area scan cameras. Line scan cameras are essential in various industrial applications, and understanding their functionality can help in selecting the right camera for specific needs.

### Why are Line Scan Cameras Needed?

Customers utilize line scan cameras for several reasons, primarily due to the following factors:

1. **Characteristics of the Target Object**: Line scan cameras are ideal for capturing moving, infinitely long objects such as fabrics, steel strips, film materials, and paper. They are also suitable for products of varying sizes, colors, and distributions on conveyor belts, including fruits, vegetables, ores, and metal parts. Additionally, they can effectively capture cylindrical objects like beverage cans and roller devices.

2. **High Requirements for Light Uniformity**: Line scan light sources can achieve more uniform illumination compared to area scan light sources, which is crucial for accurate imaging.

3. **Cost Considerations**: Using a single line scan camera can be more cost-effective than deploying multiple area scan cameras. This is particularly relevant in high-resolution flat panel inspection industries, where the costs associated with high-resolution area scan cameras can be prohibitive. Line scan cameras can significantly reduce these costs while maintaining image quality. The physical characteristics of screens, which are typically uniform in length and width, further enhance the suitability of line scan cameras for these applications.

### What is a Line Scan Camera?

As the name suggests, a line scan camera captures images in a linear format, producing a single line of pixels at a time. This differs fundamentally from area scan cameras, which capture an entire frame in one exposure. The core difference lies in the type of sensor used in line scan cameras, which is designed to capture one line of pixels at a time.

### How Does a Line Scan Camera Work?

In a line scan camera, each exposure results in the capture of one line of an image, which is taken by a row of the CMOS sensor. The final output image is constructed by processing multiple lines captured sequentially. This allows for the creation of a full image that can achieve high resolution, comparable to that of area scan cameras.

### Differences in Capture Methods

The method of capturing images differs significantly between line scan and area scan cameras. While area scan cameras capture a complete frame in one go, line scan cameras continuously capture lines as the target moves. This allows for high-speed imaging of long objects without the need for multiple camera setups.

### Advantages of Line Scan Cameras Over Area Scan Cameras

The primary advantages of line scan cameras include:

- **Cost Efficiency**: Lower overall costs for capturing long objects compared to using multiple area scan cameras.
- **High Light Uniformity**: Better control over lighting conditions, leading to improved image quality.
- **Flexibility**: Ability to capture various types of objects, including those that are continuously moving.

### How to Choose a Line Scan Camera

Selecting the right line scan camera involves several steps:

1. **Calculate Resolution**: Determine the required resolution based on the width of the object and the minimum detection precision. For example, if the width is 1800 mm and the required precision is 1 mm, you need at least 1800 pixels.

2. **Determine Pixel Precision**: Calculate pixel precision by dividing the width by the number of pixels.

3. **Determine Line Frequency**: Calculate the ideal line frequency based on the speed of the moving object and the pixel precision.

4. **Select the Camera**: Choose a line scan camera that meets or exceeds the calculated specifications for resolution and line frequency.

### Conclusion

Line scan cameras are a powerful tool in industrial imaging, offering significant advantages in cost, light uniformity, and flexibility for capturing long, moving objects. Understanding their principles and applications can help businesses make informed decisions when selecting imaging solutions for their specific needs.
