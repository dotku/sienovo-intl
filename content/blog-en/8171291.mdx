**I. Simple Matching Algorithm**

For example: To search for T=”abcabd” in string S=”abcabcabdabba” (we can assume 0-based indexing): first compare S[0] and T[0], then S[1] and T[1]... We find that they are equal until S[5] and T[5] are compared and found to be unequal. As shown in the figure:

![](https://pub-048dcb96257f476697b113fcb5939cb9.r2.dev/blog/8171291/01_9e2d7a511327402bbc7959c84ebd6f98.jpg)

When such a mismatch occurs, the T index must backtrack to the beginning, the S index backtracks to the start of the current comparison, then increments by 1, and the comparison restarts. As shown in the figure:

This time, a mismatch occurred immediately, the T index again backtracked to the beginning, the S index incremented by 1, and the comparison restarted.

The worst-case scenario for the simple matching algorithm is as follows: assuming the source string S has m characters and the target string T has n characters, the worst case involves (m-n)*n comparisons, which occurs when a match is found at the very end of the source string. How can we propose a more "intelligent" and "memory-efficient" algorithm? Let's look at the renowned KMP algorithm proposed by D.E.K. and others.
